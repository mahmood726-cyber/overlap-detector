Dear Editor,

We are pleased to submit our manuscript entitled **"Study Overlap Across 501 Cochrane Systematic Reviews: Quantifying Non-Independence for Umbrella Reviews and Methodological Research"** for consideration for publication in *Research Synthesis Methods*.

**Why this work matters.** Umbrella reviews and overviews of reviews are increasingly common study designs, yet the extent of primary study overlap across large collections of systematic reviews has rarely been quantified empirically. Study overlap creates non-independence that can bias pooled estimates and inflate confidence, but without data on its prevalence, researchers cannot judge the severity of the problem. Similarly, methodological meta-research studies that use collections of systematic reviews as independent data points -- for example, to benchmark heterogeneity estimators or publication bias methods -- implicitly assume that the reviews provide independent observations. Our study provides the first large-scale empirical assessment of this assumption.

**What we found.** We mapped all primary studies across 501 Cochrane systematic reviews in the Pairwise70 dataset, identifying 10,006 unique studies and computing the Corrected Covered Area (CCA) index, pairwise Jaccard similarity coefficients, and study frequency distributions. The CCA was 0.0001 ("Slight"), with only 4.4% of studies appearing in more than one review and only 0.5% of review pairs sharing any studies. These findings validate the independence assumption for this widely used methodological dataset, while identifying 604 review pairs where overlap should be considered.

**Why Research Synthesis Methods.** This work sits squarely within the journal's scope of advancing the methodology of evidence synthesis. The CCA and Jaccard indices are directly relevant to the journal's readership of methodologists and systematic reviewers. Our findings provide practical guidance for both umbrella review authors (who should quantify and account for overlap) and meta-researchers (who can use the Pairwise70 dataset with confidence). The study also demonstrates a reproducible pipeline for overlap assessment that can be applied to other review collections.

**Novelty and impact.** To our knowledge, this is the largest empirical assessment of study overlap across Cochrane systematic reviews, and the first to compute CCA and Jaccard indices for all possible pairs in a collection of this size. The open-source pipeline and data are available for reuse by other researchers.

The manuscript has not been published or submitted elsewhere. All authors have approved the submission and agree to be accountable for the work.

We look forward to the editorial assessment and are happy to address any queries.
